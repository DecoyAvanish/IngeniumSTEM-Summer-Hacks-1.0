What Is A 401(k)?

A 401(k) is a retirement savings vehicle offering tax benefits, named after a section of the U.S. Internal Revenue Code.

It is an employer-sponsored, defined contribution plan where employees can save for retirement. Employers may optionally match employee contributions, and in some cases, this matching is mandatory.

There are two primary types of 401(k) plans: traditional and Roth:

  1. In a traditional 401(k), employee contributions are made with pretax income, reducing taxable income in the contribution year, but withdrawals during retirement are subject to income tax.
  2. Conversely, contributions to R oth 401(k)s are made with after-tax income, offering no tax deduction in the contribution year, but withdrawals, known as qualified distributions, are tax-free.

How Does A 401(k) Work?

Introduced in the early 1980s, traditional 401(k) plans enable employees to contribute a portion of their salary before taxes, subject to specific caps.

When employees enroll in a 401(k), they commit to allocating a percentage of each paycheck directly into an investment account. Employers frequently match a portion or all of these contributions, and employees typically have a range of investment options to select from, usually involving mutual funds.

How To Start A 401(k)?

  1. Get in touch with your employer to inquire about the availability of a 401(k) plan and whether they offer a company match. If a 401(k) is offered, your employer will guide you through the process of enrollment with the necessary paperwork.
  2. Once enrolled, you'll have the opportunity to select your investments from a range of options that span from conservative to aggressive. A popular choice is the target date account, which adjusts its asset allocation based on a predetermined retirement date, becoming more conservative as you approach retirement age.
  3. If you're self-employed or operate a small business with your spouse, you might qualify for a solo 401(k) plan, also known as an independent 401(k). These plans enable independent contractors to save for retirement independently. You can establish a solo 401(k) through various online brokerage platforms.

How Does A 401(k) Make You Money?

Contributing to your 401(k) directs your funds into investments chosen from options provided by your employer. These typically include various stock and bond mutual funds, along with target-date funds that adjust the investment mix to reduce risk as retirement approaches. According to Lazaroff, target-date funds are considered one of the safer investment choices available, adjusting their allocation of stocks, bonds, and other assets as the target date nears.

Factors influencing your 401(k)'s growth include your annual contributions, potential employer matches, investment performance, and the time until retirement. A key advantage of a 401(k) is tax-deferred growth, meaning you delay paying taxes on gains, interest, or dividends until withdrawal in retirement. Alternatively, with a Roth 401(k), contributions are made with after-tax dollars, allowing for tax-free withdrawals upon retirement.

Starting a 401(k) early leverages the power of compounding, where reinvested earnings generate additional returns over time. This compounding effect can significantly increase your retirement savings, making regular contributions crucial for maximizing growth potential.

401(k) Withdrawls

Accessing funds from a 401(k) typically incurs taxes, making withdrawals challenging without tax consequences.

In traditional 401(k) accounts, earnings grow tax-deferred until withdrawal, at which point withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income. In contrast, Roth 401(k) contributions are made with after-tax dollars, allowing tax-free withdrawals under specific conditions. Both traditional and Roth 401(k) withdrawals are generally subject to IRS rules, requiring individuals to reach age 59½ or meet specific criteria like total and permanent disability to avoid penalties. Early withdrawals from either account type may incur an additional 10% early distribution tax on top of regular income taxes owed.

Contribution Limits

In 2023, the maximum annual contribution limit for employees under age 50 in a 401(k) is $22,500, which increases to $23,000 in 2024. If you participate in both a traditional 401(k) and a Roth 401(k), the combined contribution limit remains capped at $22,500 for 2023. Having multiple types of 401(k) accounts does not double the contribution limit. Individuals aged 50 and older can make an additional catch-up contribution of $7,500 in both 2023 and 2024.

Most 401(k) plans include safeguards to prevent exceeding the annual maximum contribution limit. If you inadvertently exceed this limit, you have until April 15th of the following year to withdraw the excess contributions without penalty. Failing to correct this mistake could result in double taxation—once on the excess contributions in the current year and again upon withdrawal.
